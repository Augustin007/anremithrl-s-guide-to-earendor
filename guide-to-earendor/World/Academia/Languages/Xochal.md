## 1. Introduction

Endonym: _Xochan_ (lit. “speech of flowers”)  
Exonym: _Garakic_ (by Velorian scholars, after the Empire of Garakhel).

Spoken across the lake-cities of Garakhel, _Xochan_ descends from the ancient _Teotlanic_ ritual tongues used in priestly ceremony during the Age of Shadows.  
Over the ages, the speech of the priests softened under the influence of trade, poetry, and song, until its consonants melted and its vowels lengthened into melodious flow — producing the modern language of the Garakhelian heartlands.

Two registers endure:

- Xochan-Tzin — the sacred, formal tongue, used in rites, oaths, and magic.
    
- Xochan-Común — the everyday, courtly and mercantile form, used in speech and writing.
    

This document preserves both, as the _Academy of Xochamatl_ decrees.

## 2. Phonology

### 2.1. Phoneme Inventory

|Type|Xochan-Tzin (Ritual)|Xochan-Común (Common)|Notes|
|---|---|---|---|
|Stops|p, t, k, kw, tl|p, t, k, c, l|_tl → l_ in Común|
|Fricatives|s, ʃ (x), h|s, x, j|_x_ often [ʃ] or [x]|
|Nasals|m, n|m, n|unchanged|
|Liquids|l, r|l, r|_r_ trilled in Tzin, tapped in Común|
|Approximants|w, j|gu, y|_hu → gu, cuauh → cua_|
|Vowels|a, e, i, o, u|a, e, i, o, u, ai, ia, io, ua|diphthongs emerge|

### 2.2. Phonotactics

- Preferred structure: (C)V(C)
    
- Ritual words may end in clusters (_-tzin_, _-tl_), while Común forms drop them (_-ezín_, _-l_).
    
- Stress usually falls on the penultimate syllable in Común, final in Tzin.
    

### 2.3. Orthography

The modern Garakhelian alphabet (Decree of Tlalón III, 5012 AS) uses 23 letters.  
Ritual inscriptions maintain glyphic pictograms carved on obsidian slabs.  
Common texts employ cursive script on bark-paper (_amatl_).

|Letter|Sound|Example|
|---|---|---|
|X|[ʃ] / [x]|_Xochila_|
|Tl|[tɬ] / [l]|_Tlalra / Tlara_|
|Hu / Gu|[w] / [ɣ]|_Huizilo → Guizilo_|
|Cu / Cua|[kw]/[kwa]|_Cuauhiztli → Cuaro_|

### 2.4. Prosody

Ritual chant employs strict meter: trochaic tetrasyllables.  
Everyday speech is lilting, with vowel length and rhythm reminiscent of Garakhelian song-speech (_nahuica canto_).

## 3. Morphology

### 3.1. Word Structure

Agglutinative roots joined by relational morphemes:

_Root_ + _Descriptor_ + _Honorific_  
e.g. _Cuauh-iztli-tzin_ → “Lord Obsidian Eagle”  
In Común: _Cuaro-Ezín_

### 3.2. Nominal Morphology

|Category|Suffix|Example|
|---|---|---|
|Feminine|-a / -ra|_Xochila, Atlara_|
|Masculine|-o / -ro|_Cuaro, Itzelio_|
|Plural (animate)|-me / -mes|_Xochilames_|
|Possessive|prefix _no-_|_no-atl_ → “my water”|
|Honorific|-tzin / -ezin / -rico|_Teopixqui-tzin → Teomaya Ezín_|

### 3.3. Verbal Morphology

Verbs mark aspect rather than tense.

|Aspect|Marker|Gloss|
|---|---|---|
|Progressive|prefix _ca-_|_ca-tlahoa_ → “is speaking”|
|Perfective|suffix _-ka_|_tlahoa-ka_ → “has spoken”|
|Imperative|stem + _-n_|_Tlahoan!_ → “Speak!”|
|Subjunctive|prefix _ti-_|_ti-tlahoa_ → “may speak”|

### 3.4. Derivation

Common suffixes:

- -lia (abstract nouns) → _citlalia_ “starness”
    
- -ria (action/result) → _miquiria_ “dying”
    
- -nez (agentive) → _tlatoanez_ “speaker, ruler”

## 4. Syntax

### 4.1. Word Order

- Xochan-Tzin: Verb–Object–Subject (VOS)
    
    - _Tlahoa teotl tlatoani._ → “The king speaks the god’s words.”
        
- Xochan-Común: Subject–Verb–Object (SVO)
    
    - _El Tlador habla de los dioses._
        

### 4.2. Modifiers

Adjectives follow nouns (_atl azul_ → “blue water”).  
Ritual speech doubles adjectives for emphasis (_atl-atl azul-azul_ → “pure water”).

### 4.3. Questions

Formed by sentence-final particle _-ya?_ or _in...?_  
_Cuaro tlahoa-ya?_ → “Does Cuaro speak?”

### 4.4. Negation

Prefix _ah-_ or _no-_:  
_Ah-tlahoa Cuaro._ → “Cuaro does not speak.”

## 5. Semantics & Pragmatics

### 5.1. Registers

- Tzin (Ritual): used to address gods, ancestors, and nobility.
    
- Común: used in conversation, trade, and governance.  
    Switching registers incorrectly is a grave discourtesy.
    

### 5.2. Honorifics

Ritual _-tzin_ becomes _-ezín_ or _-rico_ in Común.  
Addressing nobles: _Tonarico Ezín_  
Addressing commoners: _Tonarico-ro_ (friendly) or _Tonarico-jo_ (intimate).

### 5.3. Evidentials

Particles mark source of information:

|Evidential|Gloss|Example|
|---|---|---|
|_-qui_|direct witness|_Tona-qui_ “I saw it”|
|_-mi_|hearsay|_Tona-mi_ “It is said”|
|_-la_|divine revelation|_Tona-la_ “Revealed by gods”|

## 7. Texts & Examples

### 7.1. Interlinear Example (Ritual → Common → Trade)

Ritual (Tzin):  
_Teotl-eztli, teotl-tonah, teotl-tlalli._

```
teotl-eztli   teotl-tonah   teotl-tlalli
god-blood     god-sun       god-earth
```

Translation: “The gods dwell in blood, in sun, in earth.”

Common (Común):  
_Los dioses están en la sangre, en el sol, en la tierra._

Trade Pidgin (Low-Garakic):  
_Dios en sangre, sol, tierra._

### 7.2. Poem from the Temple of Xochamatl

> _Atl canta, luna brilla,_  
> _Flor de obsidiana respira._  
> _Cuando muere el sol, renace el canto._

Gloss:  
The water sings, the moon shines,  
The obsidian flower breathes.  
When the sun dies, song is reborn.

## 9. Notes for Learners

1. Respect register: never greet a noble in Común form.
    
2. Vowels sing: each vowel must be clear and open.
    
3. Metaphor is truth: direct speech is considered crude; every sentence is a flower.
    
4. Duality: Every concept has its _shadow word_, used in ritual inversions (e.g., _atl_ “water” ↔ _atl-micta_ “drought”).
