**Birth and Early Life**

Niamh Moonwatcher was born in the year 5380 AS to King Aodhan Moonwatcher, Lord of Trixail, and his wife Mairéad. Niamh was the third child of her household. The eldest was her sister Saoirse, four years older, and the heir was Cormac, ten cycles of the moon her elder. The fourth child was Róisín, eight years younger.

As a princess, she was given the full education befitting her station. She was instructed in music, courtly etiquette, and swordsmanship, but more notably, she showed an early aptitude for matters of governance and law, having great memory and capacity with words. Unlike her siblings, she was a compulsive rule follower, and praised for it, only failing in being too inflexible with the rules. 

Niamh was especially close to Saoirse, and had expected to remain with her sister her whole life. The two were rarely apart in childhood, and much of Niamh’s early disposition—open, curious, but guarded—was shaped by that bond.

**The Fern War**

This changed with Saoirse’s marriage.

In an effort to secure peace with the rising power of Rymfel, especially hoping for peace knowing that there was unrest within the country, King Aodhan arranged for Saoirse to be wed to the Rymish Lord Hjan, an old and corrupt man of much money and influence who ran one of the many criminal empires in Rymfel. 

At eight years old, Niamh was angry at this, and rather vocal about her opposition, and when Saoirse died not a year later, in a miscarriage, Niamh became quiet and sullen, and ceased speaking to her parents altogether. Her parents did not attempt to amend this, being preoccupied with the growing unrest, and thinking that there would be time to heal.

This was not the case, and a few months later, the Fern Uprisings culminated under the leadership of Oisin Donchadd. What began as scattered unrest escalated into open revolt, and in a decisive act, Donchadd’s followers breached the royal palace in an assault that killed King Aodhan and Queen Mairéad. 

Niamh survived only through the intervention of Gethin Dòmhnall, captain of the Kingsguard. Utilizing a network of concealed passageways—remnants of older wine smuggling routes beneath the palace—he evacuated the children: the nine-year-olds Cormac and Niamh along with the infant Róisín, and brought them to safety under the protection of Lord Breandan.

With the death of the king and the heir still a child, Trixail collapsed into civil war.

The war that followed lasted five years and was defined less by decisive battles than by shifting allegiances and fragile coalitions. During this time, the Moonwatcher children were raised largely under the care of Gethin Dòmhnall, who assumed both a protective and instructive role. It was in these years that Niamh’s political instincts were sharpened—not in courtly theory, but in necessity.

Cormac, though rightful heir, lacked the authority to unify the realm alone. The loyalties of the lords were divided among claimants. 

Bren Cathal, their older cousin on their father's side, who was a claimant to the throne, died in battle, and Cormac siezed the opportunity and arranged a marriage with Monya, Bren's younger half-sister and the daughter of Lord Cathal, in exchange for House Cathal's aid., unifying two of the largest claimants. 

Cormac similarly tried to marry his sister off to Lord Neren, but Niamh did not want to and Gethin stepped in to forbid it.

Throughout the war, Niamh maintained her refusal to enter into any political marriage, despite the strain this placed upon their position. The decision caused friction with Cormac, but Gethin defended Niamh on this matter.

In the end, Niamh and Cormac conceived what would become known as the Ruaidhrí Proclamation, which granted a number of concessions to a number of major parties and lords and appeased the Fern party by introducing a parliament, for which Oisin was elected head. Niamh refused to forgive Oisin, but Gethin forbade her from having him assassinated. 

Lord Almack, who had supported the Moonwatcher claim, sought to leverage the young king’s position for his own influence. Niamh noticed this and his attempts to assert control were curtailed with Gethin's help. 

Cormac in this time apologised to Niamh for trying to marry her off, and their larger friction was reconciled.

Niamh would blame herself for not ever reconciling with her parents.

Less than a year after the coronation, in 5395 AS, when Niamh was sixteen, Trixail entered a new conflict.

Tahlia Tormund began a campaign along the border regions, initially probing for resistance, and finding little coordinated defense, escalating to a full invasion. Trixail, still recovering from the civil war, was unable to provide an effective response. 

Within two weeks, the armies had reached the capital and the city was stormed. Gethin was gravely wounded defending Cormac in the ensuing battle, and brought inside to recover. Niamh refused to leave and helped coordinate the defense, intending to go down with her country, sending her sister away along with the Queen and the infant prince and a contingent of guards. The secret exit was known, and the Queen and prince were killed in an ambush, and Róisín taken hostage. 

Cormac attempted to lead a counter offensive and was killed, and his head was paraded on a spike outside the walls. 

Niamh coordinated secret letters while she led the defense against the siege, and slowly but surely the tide turned, and seeing a possibility for a victory and to recover her sister, agreed to marry Lord Almack in exchange for his backing. She was in deep conflict over this.

But the tide turned again, and Niamh was betrayed by Lord Neren, who she had once scorned. As Niamh ceded more and more territory, seeing the inevitable slow war of attrition, her inevitable loss, hurt by this betrayal, she angrily and thoughtlessly broke off her engagement with Lord Almack days before the wedding. He left with his forces, and many left with him. Her move cost her most of her remaining support, and ensured his lasting enmity.

Niamh was forced to negotiate a costly surrender, leaving her house nearly destitute. 

**Kordin and Róisín**

Trixail did not burn as it had in the days of the siege, nor did armies march openly through its streets, but its fate was now decided in rooms where Niamh was no longer the only voice, and often not the strongest. Lords came and went from Rymfel, each bearing claims, promises, veiled threats, and contracts written in language deliberately difficult to contest. What remained of Trixail was not divided by war, but by agreement. Niamh floundered as she attempted to consolidate everything and to keep as much power as she could. In this desperate pursuit, she found herself in horror making an agreement with Tahlia to marry off her younger sister to a Rymish lord by the year's end, and to name her the successor, protecting Niamh herself from marriage, but selling her sister in the same way her parents once had, and she spent much time rationalising it to herself, pretending it was for the good of her people.

Róisín had been returned months after the surrender, quiet now, and frightened of everything, instead of the boisterous child, alive but not unharmed, covered in scars of torture. And Niamh did not talk to her, and Róisín stayed in her room, venturing only occasionally to the garden, spending her life in constant terror, flinching at everything, hardly eating and sleeping. She said no word when she was told she was to be married off.

The suitors came immediately. 

They did not resemble (to Niamh's mind) the noble alliances of older days. The court of Rymfel was not the court of Trixail. These were men of Rymfel—lords who made it there by coin, by blood, or by violence, though most often all three. Some came openly, with retinues and banners; others sent emissaries, or offers written in careful script, proposing terms. None cared for Róisín except for the heirs she might provide that would give them power over Trixail and its resources. Niamh considered each of them, weighing the what she would get in return, fighting off her growing guilt. Niamh did not seek out Gethin, and mostly shut him out when she could as he was recovering from his injury, she felt she could not bear his imagined judgement, and hated him for trying to encourage her to help her sister. Gethin thus, instead, chose to spend his time with Róisín.

Other political matters were afoot, and so Niamh hardly payed much mind to some of the smaller retinues with smaller deals and details, and so it was that Kordin Black and his niece, Lady Jessica, arrived in the court under the pretext of securing a marriage with some former Trixalli lord who had died of illness several months prior. Jessica left but Kordin remained, pretending to try to secure the same trade arrangements with the house. 

This was, of course, a cover for his true mission. Lord Amquos Black, baron of Blackton, the secret leader of the Hidden Thieves sent out his daughter and his younger brother Kordin to overlook the situation, and now that Jessica had returned with stolen documents, Kordin remained to scout out the secret passageways and vaults and valuables for a heist at a later date. Amquos gave explicit orders to not vye for Róisín, as (Amquos said) Kordin was a terrible liar and too honest for his own good, and would do best if he avoided notice.

Kordin, though, had always been more kind-hearted than his brother, and indeed had a penchant for honesty that Amquos hated, and though he did not engage in the crueler work, he knew history and could be quiet, and Amquos beat him into submission. But as Kordin scouted the palace, he watched the proceedings deep concern, watching Róisín be brought out, on occasion, trying to ignore the desire to help, watching Niamh be cornered and exploited and robbed in every direction, and felt guilt.

He sabotaged the efforts of the worst of her suitors, those he knew to be crueler, more dangerous. And then he avoided Niamh, at all costs, for he feared his brother, and feared forgetting his mission, and he tried to write back to his brother with lies and omissions so that he could stay longer. 

His work thus took longer than expected, but his reading of history books had helped him find some hidden paths here and there that none knew, and he lightly travelled the manor, gleaning all sorts of information, and mapping these out led him to stumble into Róisín alone in her private garden.

He saw her there, scared, like an animal of prey about to bolt, holding a dagger up at him, and his heart broke. His first thought was of pity, that she must be protected, and he tried to tell her she was safe, and when she made to flee, he apologised and disappeared.

His interventions grew less indirect.

He began passing information to Niamh—quietly, through intermediaries, framed as transactions. Payment was requested, though not always collected. He justified this, in his correspondence, as positioning: gaining leverage, embedding himself within the court’s internal dynamics.

It was not entirely false.

Eventually he began coming to Niamh himself, and she began to trust him. Breaking his attempts to stay out of attention. Justifying it to his brother with all sorts of lies. She tried to introduce him to Róisín but he refused to see her  He tried to convince Niamh to marry instead, as Róisín had been through too much, and it might take off the pressure, but Niamh refused, though she hesitated when he offered himself. Niamh suggested, once, instead, that he offer to marry Róisín, but he claimed that his life was not one of the safety Róisín needed.

But then *she* sought *him*. She had seen him with her sister and recognised him, and asked Gethin about him. Gethin had here discovered of their accidental meeting. The old captain did not like the unknown, and disliked all that was going on, and how little power he had to handle it. When he learned of the garden—of the unintended meeting—his first instinct was direct action. Interrogation. Clarification. Removal, if necessary.

And they met, like this. Here the two talked for a long time, much longer than either intended, and in her he saw himself, someone forced along by the tide of politics. She thanked him for trying to help but pointed out that he, too, was trapped and alone and scared. And they met again. And one time more. And so the two swiftly developed a friendship.

After that, avoidance became impossible.

He began to see her, occasionally, under Gethin’s supervision at first, and then less formally. Their conversations went deep, and quickly. She trusted him suddenly and fiercely, and he trusted her. It unsettled him, but he felt safe with her. And she felt safe with him.

But something shifted, and he slowly fell in love. And they admitted it to each other, but nipped it in the bud, as it could be dangerous for the both of them.

And time passed. And he had exhausted indirect methods. The suitors continued to come. The offers grew sharper, more precise, more binding. Time was not working in her favour, time was running out. And he realised there was only one position from which he could guarantee anything, which was to marry her. She pointed that out to him, but he hesitated all the same. But Róisín pressed the issue, and he was glad that she did, and Róisín guilted Niamh into approving it. Gethin left with them, and they were married in the last day of the year 5396.

**Klaethor Vasterra**
Klaethor was a Baron of Neros—not among its ruling oligarchs, but sufficiently entrenched within its mercantile and diplomatic networks to wield influence beyond his title. Where many who came from Rymfel dealt in coercion, Klaethor dealt in _structure_: contracts that could be enforced, agreements that could be relied upon, and negotiations that, at least in principle, concluded.

To him, Rymfel was not merely dangerous—it was opaque.

No single authority could be trusted. No agreement guaranteed continuity. Power shifted not only between actors, but between definitions. A contract signed in the morning might be void by evening, not by breach, but by reinterpretation. In such an environment, he had no safety.

He found it in Niamh. And thus it was that the two developed a long transactional relationship, in which Niamh and Klaethor mutually gained wealth and trade. The region grew again, setting up teleports and ports. With Klaethor's help, Niamh became the center for trade in and out of Rymfel. Merchants began to treat Trixail as a stable corridor, certain Rymish lords, those more entrenched with money and nobility than crime, began to prefer her involvement where long-term gain outweighed immediate exploitation. 

**Lancel Blackton**
In 5407, Kordin and Róisín Black-Moonwatcher, who had fled politics, were assassinated by the Hidden Thieves. Gethin fought off the assassins before they killed their ten-year-old son, Lancel. Shortly afterwards, Niamh adopted him, feeling horrible for her sister's death, but promptly distanced herself, unable to deal with her guilt and how similar Lancel was to Róisín. Lancel had become quiet and dark and brooding, and Gethin taught him in combat and many other skills. The Moonwatcher wealth was enormous and Lancel was able to teach himself many new things.

**Politics**
Niamh continued to throw herself into politics, allying herself with Klaethor Vasterra and with Edris Vale, a Lord in Swanward for trade arrangements, Maelis Thornweft, a Rymish lord and information broker, Torven Halvek, a former bounty hunter, Selwyn Arclain, from Veloria, Ilyra Voss, from Hatreth. She had often to contend with Almack's meddling.


**NAF**
She attended the NAF meeting at Stonefist's request, having been recommended by Klaethor. She here met and allied herself with Colossus after Bressus's assassination. She was one of the few among her allies to escape Asmodeus's takeover, having been protected by Alantothi, and having been primed to leave after hearing of the chaos caused by Faust. She was killed on her way back by Tahlia Tormund, but it was reported as a suicide.
